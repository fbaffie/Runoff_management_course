Course plan

Approximately half of the course will be focused on the basics of:

  • Hydrology: how rainfall becomes streamflow?
  • Hydraulics: how water moves in open channels and under pressure?

Before looking at two quite disctinct scenarios (in Norway):

  • Catchment scale flooding: how to deal with large-scale flooding?
  • Urban stormwater (SW) and flooding: how to best manage urbanized catchments?

Learning outcomes

  • Understand the different components in the hydrological cycle and local water balance
  • Assess how climate change affects water balance and water quality in catchments
  • Apply relevant methods for hydrologic analysis of catchments
  • Understand how relevant computer software and GIS can be used to identify flood-prone areas and potential problems arising from urban flooding and runoff
  • Knowledge of erosion, sediment transport and flood mitigation along river courses
  • Be aware of land use planning issues related to rural and urban flood management
  • Understand the challenges and possibilities related to local runoff management and provide input for resilient solutions.

Skills

  • Make simple hydrological, hydrogeological and hydraulic calculations relevant to run-off management
  • Use different measuring techniques and carry out simple dimensional calculations of rainwater harvesting and tree management systems (so called blue-green structures) for urban runoff management
  • Develop concrete plans for local runoff management

General competence

  • Assess the individual contributions to catchment hydrology from rain, snow and glacier melt and their respective timescales
  • Understand changes in catchment hydrology due to climate change and make urban areas less vulnerable to extreme events.
  • Critically evaluate their own and others’ plans, projects and reports, and communicate with specialists and the general public about issues related to urban and rural flooding
  • Consider urban runoff as a resource and a positive element in urban environments

HYDROLOGY

The water cycle

Lecture

We look at different components of the water cycle. Climate change will impact various components of the hydrological cycle. We will quickly review potential evolutions and base our first quizz on this theme.

Quiz theme for Monday 07 May:

Effects of land hydrology on atmospheric processes and climate change

Hydrological processes

We will first discuss surface hydrological processes: precipitation, snow hydrology and vegetation processes: Lecture

We will then cover subsurface processes (groundwater, soil water and runoff generation), which are important for the design of green stormwater infrastructure. Lecture

Hydrological extremes

Lecture

Quantifying floods and droughts helps design better protection / adaptation measures.

Please attend Simen’s Master project presentation on flood frequency analysis: Wednesday 09 May, 14:30.

Quiz theme for Monday 14 May:

A restatement of the natural science evidence concerning catchment-based ‘natural’ flood management in the UK

Hydrological modelling

Being able to model streamflow helps make better management decisions such as sizing of reservoirs, culverts or allowances for irrigation. We will look at different modelling concepts used in hydrology, their data requirements, forecasting abilities and limitations.

HYDRAULICS

Fluid dynamics

We will look at water in pressure pipes. This is usually the domain of drinking water only but in somes instances, SW will have to be pumped out of a depression. Alternatively, SW could be used in off-grid design for micro-hydropower production or gravity fed irrigation schemes.

Quiz theme for tuesday May 22:

NVE report: Identification of areas exposed to flooding in Norway at a national level

Open channel hydraulics

How much flow can an open channel convey?

How high will the water level be for a specifc discharge rate and slope?

Those questions are important when sizing infrastructure or defining the extend of flood zones.

Soils, erosion and deposition

Water can mobilize particules of various sizes. This has to be taken into account in the design of hydraulic structures.

In extreme cases, boulders can be taken with the flow and form devastating debris flows. On the other hand, the smallest suspended particules are those that carry most pollution in SW runoff

Urban hydraulics

We will try to summarize how hydrology and hydraulic come into play for the design of (green) SW infrastructure.

This will involve simplified calculations for small catchments.

CATCHMENT SCALE FLOODING

Flood mapping

We saw how precipitation led to runoff and how water level in channels varies as a function of discharge. Those results are used to produce flood zoning maps. We will look at different methods to do so and discuss specific cases of vulnerability to flooding.

Quiz theme for monday May 28: Send me 4 questions and answers on your group project, and 2 on your second project.

Flood protection works

Based on flood zoning maps and the quantification of hydrological extremes in specifc catchments, protection measures can be designed. Those consist of riverbank engineering features such as dikes, diverting channels.

Based on last year’s excursion, we will look at a simple design during an exercise session

This session will consist of a full-day excursion with Svein Vågane from NVE Førde. We will look at flash flooding that occured in Utvik last autumn

Water resources management and hydropower

Even Loe from Statkraft Gaupne will take us to Jostedal to present us the hydropower infrastructure in the valley.

We will see that the hydro-electrical scheme was originally developed to mitigate flooding and discuss Statkraft’s responsability in this regard.

We will also visit field equipment such as meteorological and gauging stations that help optimize both production and flood protection.

Land use

We will see how alternative land uses may help mitigate catchment-scale flooding and erosion. Some agricultural practices could be applied in urban areas to link SW green infrastrure to local food production.

URBAN STORMWATER MANAGEMENT

Introduction to green SW infrastructure

Lecture by Kyrre Groven (Vestland Forskning)

We will look at the history of reticulated networks for SW and sewage. What are their limitations? Which pollution and flooding problems can be caused by SW runoff? Green SW infrastructure techniques will be introduced.

Green SW infrastructure

Lecture by Eli Heiberg (HVL)

Role of green infrastructure in urban storm water management:

Principles, measures and technology for local, open runoff management. We will also discuss the multifunctionality of green infrastructure.

Designing resilient infrastructure

We look at some good and bad design examples and try to understand the reasons behing design choices and possibly unintended consequences / uses.

Some examples focus on SW infrastructures but not all, in an effort to distinguish important design principles and constraints. This includes legibility. practicality, multi-functionnality…
